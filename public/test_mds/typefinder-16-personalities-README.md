## 2. Test Description

### 2.1 Myers & Briggs’ 16 Personalities Theory

Personality typing is a framework for **categorizing people according to their habitual ways of thinking and acting**. The most widely known type system was developed by **Isabel Briggs Myers** and her mother **Katharine Briggs** in the 1960‑s, building on the work of psychiatrist **Carl Jung**.

Myers and Briggs proposed that people can be described using **four key dichotomies**:

- **Introversion (I) vs. Extraversion (E)** – how we gain and expend mental energy  
- **Sensing (S) vs. Intuition (N)** – how we take in and trust information  
- **Thinking (T) vs. Feeling (F)** – how we make decisions and evaluate options  
- **Judging (J) vs. Perceiving (P)** – how we prefer to organize our outer world

Combining preferences on these four dimensions yields **16 distinct personality types** (e.g. INFJ, ESTP). Myers and Briggs used this model to create the original **Myers‑Briggs Type Indicator® (MBTI®)** assessment. Modern instruments such as **TypeFinder®** extend this approach with additional facet‑level scales and updated psychometric validation.

In practice, the 16‑type model suggests that **apparent randomness in people’s behavior is often predictable**, because individuals consistently favor particular ways of gaining energy, processing information, deciding and structuring their lives.

### 2.2 The 16 Personality Types (High‑Level Overview)

Each type combines four preferences into a coherent pattern. Common nicknames and themes include, for example:

- **INFP – The Healer:** imaginative idealists guided by inner values and meaning  
- **INTJ – The Mastermind:** strategic, independent problem‑solvers focused on systems and improvement  
- **INFJ – The Counselor:** creative nurturers driven to help others realize their potential  
- **INTP – The Architect:** analytical innovators fascinated by logic, models and design  
- **ENFP – The Champion:** people‑centered creators with contagious enthusiasm for new ideas and experiences  
- **ENTJ – The Commander:** decisive leaders who organize people and systems to achieve ambitious goals  
- **ENTP – The Visionary:** inventive experimenters eager to explore alternatives and challenge assumptions  
- **ENFJ – The Teacher:** idealistic organizers who mobilize others around a positive vision  
- **ISFJ – The Protector:** dependable caretakers, loyal and diligent in supporting others  
- **ISFP – The Composer:** gentle, present‑focused aesthetes who enjoy sensory experience and harmony  
- **ISTJ – The Inspector:** responsible organizers who value structure, duty and reliability  
- **ISTP – The Craftsperson:** observant troubleshooters with practical, hands‑on problem‑solving skills  
- **ESFJ – The Provider:** socially attuned helpers who take responsibility for people and traditions  
- **ESFP – The Performer:** energetic, spontaneous entertainers who thrive on interaction and fun  
- **ESTJ – The Supervisor:** practical managers who take charge of projects, processes and people  
- **ESTP – The Dynamo:** action‑oriented realists who seek excitement and immediate results

The assessment’s scoring logic maps a respondent’s answers to these type patterns, indicating both **best‑fit type** and relative clarity of each preference.

### 2.3 The Four Dichotomies in More Detail

The test operationalizes Myers & Briggs’ four dimensions as follows:

- **Introversion vs. Extraversion (I/E)** – how energy is managed  
  - Introverts gain energy from solitude or small, quiet settings and tend to be more reflective.  
  - Extraverts gain energy from interaction and active environments and tend to be more expressive.

- **Sensing vs. Intuition (S/N)** – how information is taken in  
  - Sensors focus on concrete facts and present‑moment experience, often preferring practical, hands‑on tasks.  
  - Intuitives focus on patterns, possibilities and abstract concepts, often looking toward the future.

- **Thinking vs. Feeling (T/F)** – how decisions are made  
  - Thinkers emphasize logic, consistency and objective criteria.  
  - Feelers emphasize values, empathy and impact on people.

- **Judging vs. Perceiving (J/P)** – how life is organized  
  - Judgers prefer structure, plans and closure; they like to decide and move forward.  
  - Perceivers prefer flexibility, options and spontaneity; they like to keep things open.

A person’s **four‑letter type code** summarizes their preferred style on each dimension (e.g., an Introverted, Intuitive, Feeling, Judging person is labeled **INFJ**).

### 2.4 Why Measure Personality Type?

Within Myers & Briggs’ framework, knowing your type:

- Provides a **concise language** for understanding your core preferences  
- Clarifies how you **differ from others** in energy, communication and decision‑making  
- Helps identify **natural strengths and blind spots** in work, relationships and learning  
- Supports **career choice** and role alignment by highlighting environments where your type tends to thrive  
- Improves **teamwork and communication** by normalizing differences rather than pathologizing them

Modern type assessments like TypeFinder® retain the accessible four‑letter codes while adding **facet‑level scales** and updated psychometrics, giving users both the intuitive simplicity of types and the richer detail of trait‑based profiles.
