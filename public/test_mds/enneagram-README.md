## 2. Test Description

The Enneagram is a personality typing system that describes people in terms of their **core motivations, desires and fears**. It has roots in ancient spiritual traditions and has been widely adopted in modern psychology‑adjacent contexts such as counseling, organizational development and spiritual direction.

The word “enneagram” comes from the Greek *ennea* (“nine”) and *gramma* (“something written or drawn”). The model defines **nine personality types**, arranged on a **nine‑point diagram** which shows how the types relate to and influence one another over time.

### 2.1 The Nine Enneagram Types

Each Enneagram type is defined by a core belief about how the world works, along with characteristic motivations and fears:

- **Type One – The Perfectionist**  
  Focus on being good, responsible and correct; fear of being imperfect or wrong.

- **Type Two – The Giver**  
  Focus on being needed and helpful; fear of being unlovable or unwanted.

- **Type Three – The Achiever**  
  Focus on success, image and achievement; fear of failure and worthlessness.

- **Type Four – The Individualist**  
  Focus on uniqueness and emotional depth; fear of having no identity or significance.

- **Type Five – The Investigator**  
  Focus on knowledge, understanding and self‑sufficiency; fear of overwhelm and intrusion.

- **Type Six – The Skeptic**  
  Focus on security, preparedness and loyalty; fear of being without support or guidance.

- **Type Seven – The Enthusiast**  
  Focus on enjoyment, variety and possibilities; fear of being trapped in pain or deprivation.

- **Type Eight – The Challenger**  
  Focus on strength, control and protection; fear of being powerless or harmed by others.

- **Type Nine – The Peacemaker**  
  Focus on harmony, comfort and going with the flow; fear of conflict and loss of connection.

Most people resonate with **one dominant type**, but may also show strong influence from adjacent types (wings) and connected types (arrows).

### 2.2 Centers of Intelligence (Triads)

The nine types are grouped into **three centers of intelligence**, also called triads:

- **Heart Types (2, 3, 4)** – Emphasize emotional intelligence and relationships; focus on identity, image and connection.  
- **Head Types (5, 6, 7)** – Emphasize intellectual intelligence; focus on safety, planning and making sense of the world.  
- **Body Types (8, 9, 1)** – Emphasize instinctual or “gut” intelligence; focus on autonomy, boundaries and responding to threats and opportunities.

Triads help explain why different types **process experience, make decisions and react to stress** in distinct ways, and are a key part of interpretation logic for the test.

### 2.3 Wings

Each core type has two adjacent types on the Enneagram diagram, known as its **wings** (e.g., a Nine has wings Eight and One). Wings represent **related styles** that can color the expression of the core type:

- Some people identify strongly with one wing (e.g., “4w3”), which adds nuance to the primary type.  
- Others may draw flexibly from both wings over time, using them as **accessible growth paths**.

The assessment should estimate **relative wing influence** for each respondent (e.g., strength of left vs. right wing).

### 2.4 Arrows (Direction of Growth and Stress)

On the Enneagram diagram, each type is connected by **two arrows** to other types:

- The **“against” arrow** points from a type that represents earlier, often repressed patterns that must be reintegrated for growth.  
- The **“forward” arrow** points toward a type that models more mature, balanced behavior the person can grow into.

The scoring model uses arrow connections to describe **patterns of movement under stress and in growth**, and to generate development recommendations (e.g., what qualities from connected types to cultivate).

### 2.5 Intended Benefits

The Enneagram test aims to help users:

- Understand their **core motivations, fears and values**.  
- Recognize habitual patterns in thinking, feeling and behaving.  
- Increase empathy by seeing how other types perceive the world.  
- Identify **personal growth paths** via triads, wings and arrows.  
- Apply Enneagram insights to relationships, teamwork and career decisions.

Results should provide a **primary type**, likely wing(s) and supplemental information about triad center and arrow dynamics, forming a compact but actionable Enneagram profile.
